chapters:
  - title: Public trust started before a job was posted.
    claim: Clients needed enough verification, reputation, and work evidence to choose confidently, while artisans needed public visibility without exposing direct contact details or external links.
    decision: Allow public visitors to browse limited artisan profiles, showing verification badges, work photos, completed job count, category-specific reviews, and Cape Town availability while excluding direct contact details.
    reasoning: The profile had to create confidence without encouraging off-platform coordination. It also needed to make verification specific, not imply a broad workmanship guarantee.
    evidence:

  - title: Jobs needed enough detail for remote-first pricing.
    claim: Artisans could not quote responsibly from vague job descriptions, and clients should not need a sophisticated contracting workflow just to ask for help.
    decision: >-
      Make jobs collect practical remote-first detail: description, photos,
      videos, measurements, access information, symptoms, preferred start date,
      and category-specific answers.
    reasoning: The launch product avoided pre-engagement site visits, public job boards, and complex calendars. Better upfront detail helped artisans price and schedule without creating a bloated workflow.
    evidence:
      - src: /evidence/artisanconnect/02-client-job-creation.webp

  - title: Quote comparison guided without declaring a winner.
    claim: Clients needed to compare price, materials responsibility, availability, reputation, and verification without the product pretending there was one objective best choice.
    decision: Show total price, labour, materials, proposed start date, duration, rating, completed jobs, and verification badges. Avoid labels like best or cheapest.
    reasoning: Service work involves judgment. A low price may not be the right decision if availability, evidence, scope, or reliability point elsewhere. The product should help clients reason, not flatten the decision.
    evidence:
      - src: /evidence/artisanconnect/03-quote-comparison.webp

        caption: Comparison surfaced meaningful differences without ranking away client judgment.
  - title: Payment created the engagement boundary.
    claim: Work should not begin until the engagement is funded, but the payment model had to stay understandable for ordinary service jobs.
    decision: Require every engagement to be fully funded before work begins. Release payment on completion for smaller jobs, and split release for larger jobs with start confirmation and completion.
    reasoning: This created accountability without building a complex milestone system. It also kept platform protection tied to real payment rather than cash, direct bank transfer, or manual proof-of-payment.

    claim: The product needed to know when work was complete, when money could release, and when both sides could leave a review grounded in a paid engagement.
    decision: Require a completion note and after-work photos, allow clients a review window, support disputes, and publish two-sided reviews only after paid engagements.
    reasoning: Completion evidence, payout, and reviews all rely on the same transaction record. Keeping them attached to the engagement made the marketplace more accountable than a lead-generation flow.

What shipped was a managed marketplace lifecycle: account and verification management, job management, client dashboard, payment, artisan dashboard, reviews, admin dashboard, and conversations.

The lasting judgment from this project is that trust-sensitive service products need simple surfaces and firm boundaries. The user should feel helped, not managed by a system they cannot understand.
