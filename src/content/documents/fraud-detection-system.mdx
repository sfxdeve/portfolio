chapters:
  - title: A score could raise a question, but not answer it.
    claim: Fraud detection could not stop at producing a number. Analysts also needed to know what happened, why it mattered, and what the system expected them to consider next.
    decision: Return the score with alert state, severity, reason codes, a recommended action, and the supporting transaction evidence.
    reasoning: Explanation and review became part of the proposed product rather than a reporting layer to add after the model was complete.

  - title: Risk and urgency answered different questions.
    claim: A model’s estimate of risk and an institution’s decision about urgency influence each other, but they are not interchangeable.
    decision: Let supervised models, anomaly models, and rules produce scoring outputs, then let policy derive severity using base alert type, escalation reason codes, linked-family severity, compliance severity, and dispute severity.
    reasoning: >-
      Keeping them separate left room for institutional and regulatory judgment.
      A transaction may carry a score, while an alert also carries its channel,
      related activity, account history, complaint or dispute links, and the need
      for regulatory review.
    evidence: []
  - title: Local payment behavior could not be treated as configuration.
    claim: A generic card-fraud model would miss how people in Pakistan move money across banking, wallet, agent, and RAAST-linked channels.
    decision: Support mobile app, internet banking, USSD, ATM, POS, e-commerce, wallet, branchless agent, and RAAST-linked flows. Treat alert taxonomy by fraud pattern, with channel as metadata.
    reasoning: Each channel changes the signals available, the information that may be missing, and the context an analyst needs. We kept those differences visible in the proposal instead of hiding them behind a generic transaction type.

    reasoning: An append-only history supported later review, disputes, calibration, and compliance without requiring the entire proposed platform to use event sourcing.

      Design lightweight alert and case workflows: alerts move from open to
      under review to resolved, high-severity alerts may open cases by policy,
      and related alerts can attach to a coherent case.
    reasoning: >-
      We kept the proposal small enough for a pilot while preserving the
      decisions that would matter later: re-evaluation after complaints or
      disputes, captured outcomes, analyst feedback, and a coherent history of

This was not shipped commercial work. Its value was in following the chain from a model’s output to a decision a financial institution could explain: what raised the alert, what made it urgent, what the analyst saw, and what history had to remain afterward.

The unresolved work remained deliberately visible. Calibration, institution-specific thresholds, governance, production hardening, regulatory review, and the proof required for deployment would all have to be established before making any commercial claim.
