chapters:
  - title: The question was bigger than model accuracy.
    claim: Fraud detection could not be framed only as producing a model score. The system also had to explain alerts and support reviewable operational outcomes.
    decision: Define the scoring response around risk score, alert status, severity, reason codes, recommended action, and enough evidence for downstream systems and analysts to act.
    reasoning: A score alone does not tell an analyst what happened, why it matters, or what should happen next. The exploration treated explainability and workflow as part of the product, not a reporting layer added later.
    evidence: []
  - title: Risk score and severity stayed separate.
    claim: The model's risk score and the operational severity of an alert are related but not the same decision.
    decision: Let supervised models, anomaly models, and rules produce scoring outputs, then let policy derive severity using base alert type, escalation reason codes, linked-family severity, compliance severity, and dispute severity.
    reasoning: >-
      This separation made room for business and regulatory judgment. A
      transaction can have a score, but an alert also carries context: channel,
      family, account history, complaint or dispute links, and whether
      regulatory review is required.
    evidence: []
  - title: Local channels changed the shape of the product.
    claim: The product had to account for local transaction channels instead of assuming a generic card-fraud model would cover the pilot.
    decision: Support mobile app, internet banking, USSD, ATM, POS, e-commerce, wallet, branchless agent, and RAAST-linked flows. Treat alert taxonomy by fraud pattern, with channel as metadata.
    reasoning: Channel differences matter for signals, missing metadata, review context, and recommended action. The exploration kept Pakistan calibration visible in the domain model rather than hiding it as configuration.
    evidence: []
  - title: Evidence retention became a product requirement.
    claim: Fraud operations need to reconstruct why a decision happened, not only see the current status.
    decision: Use current-state tables plus append-only audit and evidence history. Preserve scoring inputs, scoring outputs, reason codes, workflow decisions, handling history, and overrides.
    reasoning: Append-only evidence made review, dispute handling, calibration, and compliance support possible without committing to full event sourcing for the whole system.
    evidence: []
  - title: The workflow respected analyst judgment.
    claim: Analysts needed a prioritized queue and a focused detail view, not a full enterprise case-management platform.
    decision: >-
      Design lightweight alert and case workflows: alerts move from open to
      under review to resolved, high-severity alerts may open cases by policy,
      and related alerts can attach to a coherent case.
    reasoning: The exploration aimed for pilot fit. It included complaint and dispute-linked re-evaluation, feedback and outcome capture, and lightweight pilot summaries without pretending to be a complete enterprise risk platform.
    evidence: []

This was not shipped commercial work. Its value is in the product and system reasoning: what information a financial institution would need, how a score becomes an alert, how evidence stays inspectable, and how analysts act without treating model output as unquestionable truth.

What remains unresolved is deliberately visible: model calibration, institution-specific thresholds, governance, production hardening, regulatory review, and the evidence standard required before any commercial deployment claim.
