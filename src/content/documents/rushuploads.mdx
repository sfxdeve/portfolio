chapters:
  - title: Choosing a file was not the same as sending it.
    claim: A sender might pick the wrong file, change the message, or reconsider the expiry. Starting a large upload immediately would turn those ordinary corrections into wasted work.
    decision: Allow drag-and-drop or file picking, file removal before submission, title and message entry, expiry selection, and optional download limit. Start upload only when the sender clicks send.
    reasoning: The sender remained in control until the final action. Only then did the product create the pending transfer, begin the multipart upload, and prepare the share link for activation.

RushUploads shipped as a complete temporary-delivery service. A sender could prepare and share a transfer, follow its activity and earnings, and request a payout. A recipient could get the files without registering. The operating team could moderate the service and keep its commercial model running.

The project sharpened a trade-off I return to often: simplicity is not the absence of complexity. It is the result of deciding who should carry that complexity—and refusing to pass it to everyone else.
