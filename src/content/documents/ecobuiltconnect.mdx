  - title: Discovery had to carry trust, not just inventory.
    claim: Buyers needed to inspect materials with enough context to act, while sellers needed controlled ways to describe condition, sustainability attributes, price, quantity, and fulfillment expectations.
    decision: Use structured listing fields, controlled units, controlled conditions, and controlled sustainability attributes rather than letting the marketplace depend on free-text claims.
    reasoning: Finite and reclaimed supply is only useful when the buyer understands what is available, where it is generally located, what condition it is in, and why a sustainability claim is credible. Operators could review claims and evidence, but sellers remained responsible for what they asserted.

    decision: Make listing review a visibility and feedback process. Operators can give feedback, hide visible listings without deleting history, and block unsupported materials, but they do not rewrite seller-owned claims.
    reasoning: This kept accountability clear. Sellers owned their descriptions and evidence. Operators governed whether those claims were safe and supported enough to show.

What shipped was not just a marketplace surface. It was a controlled product system for seller verification, listing review, buyer discovery, checkout, seller-scoped orders, fulfillment, disputes, and quantity-based payouts.

The work reinforced a principle I keep returning to: when a product handles finite supply and trust-sensitive claims, the interface has to make responsibility visible before the edge cases arrive.
